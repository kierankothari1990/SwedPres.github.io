About me

I hold an MSc in Computer Science from the University of Bath and a BA in Politics, Philosophy and Economics from Trinity College Dublin.

I am interested in data analytics and visualisations, and the insights that can be drawn from the information and data generated by people, organisations and the commercial market.

Below is a sample of my work. Using Python, SQLite and R, I have scrapped data from the popular online game Fantasy Premier League (FPL) in order to examine the trends and patterns from within the mini-league I play in.

FPL is essentially an optimisation problem in which you must divide your assets (Â£100m) across 15 footballers who every week have the potential to accrue points which contribute towards your overall score. Over 6 million players compete in the game globally, but you can also play against friends in smaller mini-leagues. The following is a presentation of the trends and patterns extracted from one of the 2018/19 mini-leagues I play in.

The above line graph indicates that over the course of the season so far a gap of 333 points has opened up between first and last place league in the league. Between these points lie a further 6 teams whose performances across the season have overlapped and intertwined on numerous occasions. Indeed, such is the competitiveness of the league that by presenting the data in this style of simplistic line graph, it is actually quite challenging to identify the relative performance of each team.

It is far more useful to present the data for each team on a month-by-month basis set against a benchmark of the average monthly performance across the league:

As the above shows, we can now far more clearly track the relative performances of each team. We can, for example, far more easily identify that the month of December held particular significance for the fortunes of Joe and Barney. With both teams evenly matched at the end of November, Joe experienced tremendous success and took 1st place by January, whereas Barney slumped from 4th to 7th in the same timeframe.

Equally, if we toggle off the teams to just leave the data for Sam, Olly and Elliot we can see that performance of Olly's team has fluctuated widely from month to month, whereas following a disappointing start to the season, Elliot and Sam have largely trended upwards.

In understanding why certain teams have performed well or not, two factors that are worthy of analysis are firstly, the number and type of transfers each team has made, and secondly, whether teams have been effective in their captaincy decisions. (Each team is limited to one free transfer per week, with additional transfers in excess of this costing 4 points, whilst the decision of which player to captain each week is critical because this player is awarded a double score).

The above radar graph represents the number of free transfers and points hit transfers each team has made. We can clearly see that Kieran, Patrick, Elliot, Sam and Joe have all been quite risk averse in the transfer policy. Each of these teams has used their full allocation of free transfers and operated with sensible caution when choosing to spend points on additional transfers. The same, however, cannot be said for Olly and Barney, who have been wildly profligate in the transfer market.

The above bar chart represents the number of points each team has accumulated from 1] their base squad (which excludes the player selected as captain) and 2] their captain. Each bar is set against the average performance across the entire league (i.e. a score below 0 indicates a negative performance compared to the average). When it comes to effective decision making in the choice of which player to captain each week, Kieran's team has excelled. Indeed, in contrast to the second placed team in the league, Kieran has accrued an additional 54 points as a result of correctly identifying which player to captain. We can also see that although Joe is placed in 3rd overall, he has actually been quite poor in choosing which player to captain.

If we remove both the points accumulated as a result of each team's captaincy decision and additionally the points each team has lost from 'points hit' transfers, we can develop a metric which offers some insight into the relative skill of each player in the league. This is represented in the above graph in the form of a dotted line which indicates how many points each team would have scored both without captains and without losing points from 'points hit transfers'.

In order to see the data for each team, use the toggles to select which teams are to be displayed on the graph.

This metric indicates, for instance, that the decision making of Olly has largely been sup-optimal when it comes to the decisions he has made, whereas Elliot and Sam have both performed well due to their managerial choices.
